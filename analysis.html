Norman vs. GOMS Comparison: The Wi-Fi Toggle Case

Based on Dix et al., Human–Computer Interaction (Ch. 12), this table compares how two classic interaction models interpret a simple but realistic task: enabling Wi-Fi on a modern smartphone interface.

Comparison of Norman’s Action Cycle and GOMS Concepts
Norman’s Stage Task Step (iPhone / Samsung) GOMS Component Alignment Status
Goal (The Intention) "I want to connect to the internet." GOAL: Enable-Wi-Fi ✅ Direct Match
Plan (Action Sequence) Deciding to swipe down for Quick Settings. METHOD: Swipe + Tap ✅ Conceptual Match
Specify (Identify Action) Visually searching for the Wi-Fi icon. Not Explicitly Modeled ❌ Assumed by GOMS
Execute (Physical Act) Physical finger tap on the screen. OPERATOR: Tap [K] ✅ Direct Match
Perceive & Evaluate Seeing the icon turn blue and signal activation. Outside GOMS Scope ❌ Evaluation Gulf
Feedback Interpretation Understanding that Wi-Fi is now active. Implicit Assumption ❌ Weakly Represented

Theoretical Insight: Closing the Gulfs

The Gulf of Execution

In the Wi-Fi example, users unfamiliar with gesture-based interfaces may not realize they need to swipe down. This creates a large Gulf of Execution. GOMS can predict execution time once the method is known, but Norman’s model explains why users fail before execution even begins.

The Who Paid Problem

As discussed in class, tasks like splitting a bill involve shifting goals and reinterpretation of outcomes. These tasks require multiple cycles of Norman’s action model. GOMS is less suitable because it assumes stable goals and routine behavior.
